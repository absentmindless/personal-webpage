Research

Through the Vagelos Integrated Program in Energy Research (VIPER) at Penn, I've had the opportunity to conduct research with the Micro-Sensors and Micro-Actuators Group in the Singh Center for Nanotechnology.

Summer 2016

In my first summer with the group, I spent a short time on a microfluidics project developing nanostructured PDMS molds for microfluidic channels, and tested the effect that nanostructured walls had on the flow rate of liquid through microfluidic channels.

I later transitioned to a project in designing a new fabrication technique to reduce time and money required to fabricate micron-thin metal films and circuit components. We successfully demonstrated that using it is possible to reuse the seed layers upon which such films are electroplated if the layers are coated with a molecule-thin monolayer of dodecanethiol before the electroplating process. 2017-18

The rest of my research with the MSMA group involved simulating various microinductor geometries to help develop new fabrication processes. Using COMSOL Multiphysics, a physics-based simulation software which performs finite-element-analysis (FEA), I simulated microinductors of different shapes at various frequencies to determine optimal parameters that would reduce power loss. Most recently, I simulated linear microinductors with conductive polymer-mediated laminated cores to see if linear microinductor fabrication could be made more efficient by replacing the insulators in traditional laminations with conductive polymers without significantly impacting the performance of the components.
